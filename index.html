Le Pakshi —

Rise, O Bird

Origin · Mythology

"Where the sacred bird Jatayu fell wounded, where the divine breathed life back into the earth — here stands Lepakshi, born of a god's compassion."

According to Hindu mythology, Lepakshi is the hallowed ground where Jatayu — the divine eagle — collapsed after his valiant battle with Ravana to save Goddess Sita. Lord Rama, discovering the fallen bird, uttered the words "Le Pakshi" (Rise, O Bird), blessing this sacred land with its name and its legend.

Built in the 16th century under the Vijayanagara Empire by chieftains Virupanna and Viranna Nayaka, the temple complex at Lepakshi remains one of India's finest examples of Dravidian artistry — a monument where every carved surface is a prayer crystallised in time.

Monolithic Marvel

The Great Nandi

India's largest monolithic Nandi bull stands 4.5 metres tall and 8 metres long, carved from a single granite boulder. Adorned with delicate stone jewellery, this sacred guardian gazes with serene devotion toward the presiding deity.

Engineering Mystery

The Hanging Pillar

Among Lepakshi's 70 exquisitely carved pillars, one defies gravity — suspended from the ceiling without touching the ground. Visitors traditionally pass a cloth beneath it as a token of witnessing this unexplained miracle.

Learn More →
Living Murals

The Frescoes

The ceilings of Lepakshi bear some of India's finest Vijayanagara-era frescoes — vivid murals of the Mahabharata, Ramayana, and the legends of Shiva, painted in natural pigments that retain their brilliance five centuries on.

The Sacred Footprint

Sita Pada —
The Divine
Impression

Carved into the living rock of Lepakshi is a pair of giant footprints, believed by many to be the sacred imprints of Goddess Sita as she passed through this hallowed land during the events of the Ramayana.

Standing over a metre in length, the Sita Pada remains one of Lepakshi's most venerated relics. Pilgrims travel from across the subcontinent to pay homage, leaving offerings of flowers and prayer at the base of these ancient impressions.

Studies in Ink & Stone
The Sacred Manuscript

Seventeen Studies
in Ink & Stone

A visual exploration of form, shadow, and devotion — each line a meditation on the divine geometry of Lepakshi Temple, drawn in the tradition of measured architectural survey.

Studies attributed to Dr A S Raja Rao  ·  Pen & Ink on paper

Chapter I

Entry — The Outer World

The first sight of Lepakshi — the silhouette of the gopuram against open sky, the outer parikramas that circle the sacred ground.

Study I · 1

The Outer Parikramas

A single ink traverse of both circumambulation circuits — note the shift in cornice height between the first and second ring. Hover to see the temple today.

Study I · 2

Gopuram & Mandapa

The Dravidian tower in elevation — seven storeys of diminishing yali pilasters, capped by a barrel-vault shikhara unique to Vijayanagara style

Chapter II

Guardians at the Threshold

Before you enter the inner world, the dvarapalas stand watch — fierce, adorned, eternal. Veerabhadra. Ganesha. The nameless warrior with his spear.

Study II · 1

Veerabhadra

Eighteen arms bearing weapons of cosmic destruction — the gana lord born from Shiva's rage to execute Daksha's ill-fated sacrifice

Study II · 2

Dvarapala & Ganesha

Paired doorway guardians — the left figure bears a club, the right holds a lotus bud symbolising abundance and the removal of obstacles

Study II · 3

The Warrior at the Gate

A tribhanga stance softened by a relaxed hip — the warrior's posture signals potential force, not rest; vigilance without aggression

Chapter III

Figures of the Mandapa

The pillared halls hold a silent congregation — deities, apsaras, and celestial attendants carved into living stone, now alive again in ink.

Study III · 1

Deity in the Niche

The cusped archway frames a tiered mukhamandapa — a compositional device borrowed from Chalukyan prototypes and refined at Vijayanagara

Study III · 2

The Abhaya Mudra

Right hand raised, palm outward — the oldest iconographic gesture of protection in South Asian sculpture. Hover to see the hall it inhabits.

Study III · 3

Celestial Attendant

The high chignon and elongated fingers mark this as a vidyadhara — a knowledge-bearing celestial being attendant on the divine court

Study III · 4

Four-Armed Deity

Conch, discus, mace, lotus — the four cardinal attributes identifying this as a Vaishnava form within a predominantly Shaiva temple

Study III · 5

The Lion Plinth

The sardula base — half-horse, half-lion — appears on every major pillar shaft at Lepakshi, marking the full maturity of the Vijayanagara aesthetic

Study III · 6

Apsara

The apsara's weight shifts over the right hip in atibhanga — a triple curve conveying sensuous celestial grace unique to Vijayanagara figuration

Study III · 7

Apsara with Lotus

The half-open lotus — symbolising unfolding creation — is a signature motif of Krishnadevaraya's patronage at both Lepakshi and Tirupati

Study III · 8

Amid Scrolling Foliage

The kirtimukha at the arch apex consumes its own body — below it, cascading vine-work fills every surface with the horror vacui of late Vijayanagara decoration

Chapter IV

Cosmic Forms — The Sanctum

In the innermost chamber, the divine takes its most expansive form — multi-armed, multi-headed, treading the earth. Here, time folds.

Study IV · 1

Brahma — The Creator

Rarely depicted as primary deity — Lepakshi's Brahma bears a fifth head implied by the elevated lotus seat, a cosmological claim unique to this temple

Study IV · 2

The Wrathful Form

The trampled figure beneath — not enemy but ego — is a motif of inner conquest repeated on all four sanctuary pillars of the garbhagriha

Study IV · 3

Cosmic Dance

Ten arms form a perfect radial symmetry — the dance is not movement but the geometry of creation frozen in stone, legible only from a distance

Study IV · 4

Veerabhadra Supreme

The presiding deity in full regalia — hover to reveal the painted fresco above the sanctum door that served as the artist's primary reference for this study

"What the chisel wrought in stone, the pen has written in devotion."

— Dr A S Raja Rao

Morning Ritual

Pesarattu & Upma

Green moong crepes served with ginger chutney and soft upma — the canonical Andhra breakfast, eaten standing at small dhabas near the bus stop.

The Staple

Ragi Sangati

The Sweet Close

Jaggery rice pudding fragrant with cardamom and cashew — often offered at the temple first, then served in the hotel. Sacred before it is sweet.
